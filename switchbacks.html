Switchbacks | UI Case Study

Overview

Problem

Trip planning for National Parks can be time consuming when you’re interested in a more off the beaten path experience. Key resources for hikes, camping, backcountry permits and park info is often spread across multiple sites. This makes the planning experience unnecessarily complex and therefore time-consuming.

Solution

Create an App that combines all the pertinent information currently available across multiple platforms into one singular location. Allowing users a more simplified and efficient experience when planning future visits to National Parks.

The 5 W's

  • Who? Adventurous individuals who are trip planning to US National Parks.
  • What? A Native Mobile App for iOS and Android users that provides all National Park trip planning information in one spot.
  • When? Prior to visiting the parks when they are in the planning stages. On the trip itself.
  • Where? At home when they're planning their trip and in the outdoors, while at the park.
  • Why? Planning an adventurous National Park trip can be both confusing and time-consuming when resources tend to be scattered across multiple platforms.

User Flow

User Stories

  • Users should be able to quickly and easily navigate to the page of the park they're interested in visiting. Here they will be able to find inspiration, view necessary travel requirements and be able to proceed with trip planning.
  • For the planning stage, users can input important trip dates into their calendar, access a map to determine their route and store all of this pertinent information once compiled.
  • Users can conveniently refer back to past trips they've taken and all trip planning details will be made shareable so that resources can be passed along freely to fellow adventurers.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

With my User Flows defined, I sketched out 8 low-fidelity wireframes. I’ve found that a pencil and paper is the best way for me to efficiently extract design ideas from my head. I feel that I’m able to be more creative and flexible with my designs when I know I can just erase and try again if something didn’t come out how I intended.

Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

My next step was to digitize my sketches into Mid-Fidelity Wireframes for iOS and Android devices using their respective design guidelines - Human Interface and Material Design.

The more detail I added to each screen, the more I was able to determine what additional screens my users would benefit from. Therefore, my 8 screens evolved into 12 for each device.

Once my screens were digitized, I then conducted an analysis of two competitor apps offering a similar experience to Switchbacks - Chimani and National Park Trail Guides. This was an opportunity for me to understand how those companies have solved user problems and uncover any areas of improvement.

I focused my research on identifying my likes and dislikes pertaining to their design solutions and made note of which features I thought would be useful to implement in my own designs.

High-Fidelity Wireframes

With the use of content, color and imagery, my designs evolved into High-Fidelity Wireframes. It was then time to test my designs out on potential users to determine what improvements could be made prior to finalizing.

Using Figma’s prototype feature, I created an iOS and Android specific prototype that I dispersed to users of those devices and requested that they evaluate the designs. I collected all that feedback and implemented the changes accordingly to ensure that my designs are always keeping the users needs at the forefront.
